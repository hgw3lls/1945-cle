Movie Houses // Today at Your Theatre

May 8, 1945. The war in Europe is ending. Ration books still sit in kitchen drawers; gasoline and rubber remain scarce. Streetcars hum along Euclid and St. Clair, and most Clevelanders still walk to their neighborhood movie house. Those small-but-proud theatres within a few blocks of home glow again—sanctuaries where the city gathers to share light, sound, and news from a wider world finally at peace.

Reel Screens: Cleveland 1945 is your map back to that moment. The listings come from The Cleveland Press, which published the daily “Movie Houses // Today at Your Theatre” column—each dot on the map represents one of those theatres and its program for May 8, 1945. Tap a dot to see what was on the marquee, open photos and notes, and trace how theatres clustered along transit lines and commercial corridors. Compare downtown palaces with corner houses; follow double-features, cartoons, and wartime newsreels as they moved across the city’s screens.

Here on the Cuyahoga, Cleveland’s deeper cinema story comes into focus—call it Hollywood on the Cuyahoga. This was a distribution hub and a showman’s town, where ornate marquees lit Euclid Avenue and neighborhood houses kept the reels turning after factory shifts. In 1945, with V-E Day crowds spilling into the streets, the movies were more than entertainment: they were civic ritual—binding together downtown glitz and working-class blocks from Glenville to Tremont to Collinwood.
